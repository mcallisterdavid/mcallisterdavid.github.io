On Diffusion Models and 3D Generation

David McAllister

Mid-Project Writeup

Diffusion models have demonstrated breakthrough text-to-image generation quality and now power numerous AI products. They beat GANs in quality of generation and in their ability to generalize to novel text prompts. Follow-up works applied them to new settings, sped up their sampling procedure and integrated them with large language models to enable rich text-driven generation. Their application to 3D generation is still limited, however, by a tradeoff between extremely slow test-time optimization and fast generation with low quality samples.

A direct approach to 3D generation is to leverage datasets of 3D assets. The largest dataset includes over a million assets. Upon inspection, however, we observe that many of these samples are poor quality. We also observe mixed results in models trained with this data. While this data may be helpful, it is clearly not a path toward a general solution in this problem space.

Dreamfusion, another popular approach, proposes to use a pre-trained 2D diffusion model to “critque" a 3D volume via optimization until it converges to a valid sample. This is slow due to its test-time optimization nature and it leads to a dramatic blurring effect. We analyze this blurring effect by replicating it in 2D. Despite these drawbacks, this method is effective because it leverage the vast diversity of 2D image data and, by extension, 2D diffusion models. It inherits support for long-tail prompts and interpolates effectively between concepts.

Diffusion distillation literature proposes methods to speed up the sampling procedure for diffusion models (1) (2) (3), predict with classifier-free guidance (4), and apply them to new tasks such as editing or correspondence. It often centers around the influential idea of interpreting diffusion models as implicit models. We try to unify this perspective with the two outlined approaches to 3D generation and work toward a single 3D diffusion model trained on vast amounts of 2D image data. We propose to do this through diffusion distillation. Our results are preliminary but show interesting future possibilities in this space.

Background

The following is the related work that motivates this project.

DDPM

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models, or DDPM as it is usually called, showed that diffusion models could outperform GANs in image generation. It establishes two stochastic processes---the forward process and the reverse process. The forward process corrupts a data sample with i.i.d. gaussian noise according to some schedule that varies with variable t. This t is the “timestep” and indexes the stages of the forward and reverse processes. While it can theoretically be a continuous value in some defined range, it is quantized to 1000 steps. That is, t can be as high as 999 and as low as 0.

In other words, diffusion models learn the first derivative of the data's probability density function across the space of the forward process.

A trained DDPM can be sampled to produce an output in many ways. The original paper proposes a stochastic sampler based on Langevin dynamics. This sampler performs well but requires many evaluations of the learned function, which makes it prohibitively slow. More recent works resolve this issue.

DDIM

Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models, or DDIM, makes an important discovery that a DDPM model can be recast to solve an ordinary differential equation. This ODE, called the probability flow ordinary differential equation, is the first order ODE mapping i.i.d. gaussian noise to the data distribution. Intuitively, this follows from the idea that a DDPM predicts the gradient of the likelihood, which resembles a first-order ODE. The paper justifies this mathematically.

DDPM portrays the forward process as a random Markov chain between data and noise, while this ODE derivation is a non-Markovian process. The paper shows that when trained with the Markov process, the model also learns the non-Markovian process. This means that you can sample by solving the ordinary differential equation. There is a vast literature focused on solving ordinary differential equations efficiently, which can be applied to accelerate sampling. While the original DDPM paper produced samples with 1000 steps at test-time, DDIM can produce results of similar quality with 50 or even 25 steps.

While DDPM produced great results, it was computationally expensive for inference. This meant GANs were still preferred for many applications. DDIM changed this, however, as DDPMs could now be sampled efficiently. It also established a new way to think about diffusion models as a powerful self-supervised objective to learn the gradient of the likelihood. This gradient could then be thought of similarly to a discriminator in a GAN, but instead of producing a binary real/fake signal, it provides an update direction to make an image more “real."

Dreamfusion

Dreamfusion extended this intuition to use diffusion models as well-informed critics during optimization. Dreamfusion introduces a “score distillation sampling” loss that provides an update gradient for an image to map it to the natural image distribution. The gradient takes the form∇L=ϵϕ^(zt:y,t)−ϵ\nabla\mathcal{L}=\hat{\epsilon_{\phi}}(z_t:y,t) - \epsilon\text{}∇L=ϵϕ​^​(zt​:y,t)−ϵ﻿, where y is an input image, ϵ\epsilonϵ﻿ is randomly sampled i.i.d. gaussian noise, ϵϕ^\hat{\epsilon_{\phi}}ϵϕ​^​﻿ is the diffusion model's predicted score and t is uniformly sampled across its possible range. This gradient should include the Jacobian of the diffusion model, but it is omitted in the chain rule to save memory and it is observed to produce better results.

The derivation is relatively verbose, so I will not include it in this writeup, but it is derived from DDPM. The effect of this function is that diffusion models trained to produce 2D images can also update 2D images to more closely match the natural image distribution. If the input image is produced by a differentiable process, this SDS gradient can also update the parameters of that process via the chain rule.

NeRF is therefore an applicable representation for this problem. NeRFs represent a 3D volume as a function that defines density and color at each location in a defined bounding box. This can be represented fully implicitly as a multilayer perceptron or more explicitly as a large number of 3D gaussians. This representation can be projected into 2D images via volume rendering. Once again, the details are verbose but it is important to note that volume rendering can be reparameterized to be differentiable.

Since NeRFs can produce renders with gradients tracing back to a 3D volume and these renders can be critiqued by the SDS loss, the SDS loss can update the NeRF to produce natural-like images when rendered. There is an observed blurring and oversaturation that this objective produces. The blurring has since been shown to be the results of distilling a variational representation (diffusion model) into a single point distribution (NeRF), which is a mean-seeking procedure. The cartoon-ish colors are the result of high classifier free guidance, which shrinks the distribution of the diffusion model (less blurring) but leads to oversaturation. An additional problem of this method is that the SDS loss produces very noisy gradients, meaning the NeRF optimizes slowly. While the results look reasonable, it can take over an hour per 3D result and the optimization can diverge.

Progressive Distillation for Fast Sampling of Diffusion Models

Progressive Distillation for Fast Sampling of Diffusion Models proposes a procedure to “distill” a DDPM into another diffusion model for faster sampling. It follows the teacher-student paradigm where a well-trained teacher model supervises the optimization of a student model. The σt\sigma_tσt​﻿ value can be chosen, representing different forward processes that a DDPM can reverse. Importantly, it can set to 000﻿, removing all randomness from the sampling process. In this setting, the only randomness is at the beginning of the sampling procedure, when zTz_{T}zT​﻿ is sampled as a zero-mean gaussian noise vector with an identity covariance matrix. Thus, the deterministic DDIM solver forms a deterministic relationship between full samples of noise and samples of the diffusion model. When training a DDPM, there is no such deterministic relationship, which necessitates a high resoluton of timesteps and stochastic training. DDIM shows that this DDPM procedure lays out a probability flow with a convenient determinism.

Progressive Distillation proposes to learn deterministic relationships with a sufficiently parameterized model. Other concurrent works, such as BOOT and Consistency Models make this same observation and derive different training procedures. Progressive Distillation's method fine-tunes a DDPM with a copy of itself as the teacher. The teacher takes two DDIM steps and the student learns to replicate the result in a single step. While the DDPM needs high resolution of timesteps to solve the ODE, the distilled model can learn the deterministic path that comes from solving two timesteps.

This procedure is done recursively, halving the number of steps each time. That is, a 1000 step model can be distilled to 500 steps then a 500 step model can be distilled to 250 steps and so on. Eventually this procedure diverges as it saturates the capacity of the student model, but it can produce convincing samples in as few as 4 steps. Follow-up works combine this sort of procedure with a discriminator to get samples with only a single step.

Multidiffusion

Multidiffusion is a method for combining multiple diffusion predictions to stitch together generated images.

It first defines a canvas that is larger or in a different aspect ratio than a standard text-to-image generation. This canvas is then populated as a single, large zTz_{T}zT​﻿ of zero-mean i.i.d. gaussian noise. The method runs a reverse process on this large noise sample by splitting it up into overlapping crops. Each of these crops is a square---the native aspect ratio and resolution of the diffusion model---so this crop of noise is in-distribution for the diffusion model. Crucially, this is pixel-aligned, so the noise does not go through a warp or resampling that could disrupt its i.i.d. nature (bilinear interpolation of an i.i.d. noise map is no longer i.i.d.). The method then averages the zt−1z_{t-1}zt−1​﻿ predictions generated by each crop, resulting in a full canvas zt−1z_{t-1}zt−1​﻿. This is a single step in the reverse process, which can be performed iteratively to produce a natural image sample.

Stochastic Multidiffusion

We perform the exact procedure defined in Multidiffusion with one modification---we use a stochastic sampler rather than a deterministic one. Multidiffusion uses the DDIM deterministic sampling procedure with the stochastistic coefficient set to 000﻿. We switch to the DDPM ancestral sampler, which samples new noise at each step in the reverse process. We define the Multidiffusion crops to be over a single square patch, as we do not observe a difference from the actual Multidiffusion setting in experiments. Building Toward Better 3D Generation

Consistent Noise

These 2D experiments replicate the shortcomings of current 3D generative approaches. Dreamfusion exhibits the same blurriness and lack of high spatial frequency detail in 3D and 2D. An obvious solution to this is to replicate the noise canvas that drives Multidiffusion's success. It is impossible, however, to resample, warp or project samples of 3D gaussian noise to 2D without violating the i.i.d. property. We experimented with various implementations but the conclusion was the same---there is no universal solution to this problem.

The simplest example of this effect can be demonstrated by rescaling high resolution noise to a slightly lower resolution via bilinear interpolation. SDS Diffusion Distillation

SDS was formulated from diffusion distillation objectives. While it struggles to distill into a non-variational objective (image or volume), no works have attempted to use it for distillation into a student diffusion model. We must modify previous distillation works, however, to make our method applicable to the 3D context. There are two major constraints.

  1. We do not have access to ground truth 3D data so it must be data-free
  1. Noise cannot be shared between student and teacher for the reasons outlined above

To experiment, we adopt the standard SDS objective.

We define x^0,3D\hat{x}_{0,\text{3D}}x^0,3D​﻿ as the 3D diffusion model's denoised prediction at timestep ttt﻿. We perform this optimization across a resolution of 500 timesteps and cache intermediate results to reduce computational cost. To validate the method we performed two experiments.

Our first experiment was to test this objective's plausability in 2D. We were able to achieve reasonable results training on 4 Titan RTX GPUs for one day. We have not determined if the softness is inherent to the method or due to our computational resource limits. We sample from the resulting model's reverse process and share the results above.

We also performed an overfitting experiment to optimize a single reverse process in 3D. In this procedure, we optimize a volume at every step of the reverse process to match the denoised predictions from a teacher model. We use a 2D teacher model finetuned on 3D assets to remove camera ambiguities, simplifying the experiment. This experiment has the same SDS objective but does not blur as severely since the distribution of the finetuned 2D teacher model is much smaller than one trained on natural images. This experiment validates that our objective works across the 2D-3D boundary via differentiable rendering. This would not be true of any method that involves projecting or resampling noise.

Future Work

Our experiments suggest a path toward a 3D diffusion model trained from knowledgeable 2D teachers. Important questions remain, including 3D architecture, compute resources needed and autoencoding schemes to accelerate the diffusion model. The value of such a model is also uncertain. Video diffusion models are improving rapidly and include conditioning for camera movements. A perfect video model would solve the novel view synthesis problem and a 3D volume could be optimized to match its outputs. The effectiveness of this approach remains to be seen, however.
